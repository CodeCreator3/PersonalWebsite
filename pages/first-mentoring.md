# FIRST Mentoring

## Roarin' Robotics Northshore Christian Academy

In middle school, I attended Northshore Christian Academy. They had a few First Lego League robotics team called the Robogators. I joined Blue team #6438. Through this program I gained valuable problem solving and teamwork experience.
<br>

In sophomore year of high school, I came back to that FLL program as a mentor. At this point, the school mascot had changed, so they became the Roarin' Robotics. Being a mentor was a new experience for me; instead of coming up with the ideas myself and doing work myself, I had to guide students through the same process. Additionally, I worked through conflicts between the students and built up their [core values](https://www.first-lego-league.org/en/2023-24-season/challenge-resources/core-values).
<br>

I continued mentoring through the rest of high school and became valuable to the program. I was even nominated by the students for the mentor award at an event and given an award by the Roarin' Robotics program for being a role model to the students.

## 11025 Garage Gang

Through my association with Big Sky Robotics, I learned about a new FRC team being started in the Bozeman area: #11025 Garage Gang. Garage Gang is the second FRC team in Montana. 
<br>

I decided to come to a meeting to see if I could give any advice based on my software experience. Pretty soon after, I decided to become a mentor. I made this decision, despite a pretty busy schedule, because I saw some serious determination in both the students and mentors on that team. Not only did they have a rediculously complex and competitive robot in the works, they were doing a lot of outreach in the community to improve FIRST's presence in Montana with the goal of creating a stronger community of FIRST in Montana.

![alt text](assets/first-mentoring/IMG_2503.png)

That year, I taught multiple students and mentors how to write FRC software, came to two Garage Gang meetings every week, and went to their activites and competitions.
<br>

At Garage Gang's first competition in Boise Idaho, they won the Rookie Allstars award for their impact on their community as a new team. The robot didn't work as intended due to several technical issues, but the students had a lot of fun and learned a lot. I even taught a student how to write an autonomous routine the day before the event. Here is a clip of their autonomous routine.

Additionally, our head mentor, Drew Currie, won the Woodie Flowers Finalist award. The Woodie Flowers Finalist award is given to a single mentor and is very difficult to get due to tough competition. According to the [Woodie Flowers website](https://www.woodieflowers.org/), "The Woodie Flowers Award celebrates effective communication in the art and science of engineering and design." I am incredibly proud of Drew for achieving this and fully believe he deserves it.
